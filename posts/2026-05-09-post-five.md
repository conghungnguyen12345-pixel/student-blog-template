## Introduction

Our team's general idea of our target audience for Critique Canvas is any artist who wants accurate critiques of their artwork. This idea was then showcased to our tutor, where he pointed out an issue where our definition is too broad, and that we should be more detailed on our user community.

## Reconsidering the Community

Our initial instinct was to keep the community broad. Painters, illustrators, photographers, digital artists, and cartoonists all share the same core problem: feedback on visual work is vague and disconnected from the actual image. A comment like "the lighting feels off" means nothing without knowing which part of the image it refers to. This is true regardless of what type of visual art is being discussed. The annotation feature works identically for all of them.

After reflection, we concluded that our community is already well defined — it is anyone who creates visual art and wants spatial, precise critique. The annotation feature does not need to work differently for a photographer versus an illustrator. The pain point is the same, and the solution is the same.

## What We Decided to Add Instead

Instead of forming varieties of areas for single types of artists, which will make our work messier, our team will implement categories of distinct artworks in the upload form. When uploading, a user selects the type of art they are sharing — Photography, Illustration, Digital Art, or Traditional Art. This allows the gallery to be filtered by medium without requiring separate pages or separate communities.

This decision came from thinking carefully about what our community actually needs. Artists browsing the gallery may want to find work similar to their own medium. A photographer looking for inspiration is more likely to engage with other photography than with digital illustrations. Adding categories serves this need with a small, targeted change rather than a large structural one.

## Our lesson on scope

This obstacle provided a valuable lesson in managing the scope of a project. One of the first idea our team came up with is to create distinct sections for different types of artists, which will cause our project to be more complex and confusing. One of our team members, Mehak, provided a potential solution for implementing categories, which is more logical since it can still tackle the issue, and our team does not need to do additional work.

High-quality scope management is to provide a non-complex solution that can effectively tackle an obstacle. Implementing a dropdown and filter will provide similar results to distinct sections, but will be less time-consuming and ensure that the code will be clean and presentable.
